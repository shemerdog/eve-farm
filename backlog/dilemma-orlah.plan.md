# Dilemma — עָרְלָה (Orlah) — Forbidden First Fruits

## What It Is

A tree's fruit during its first three years is forbidden — it may not be eaten, sold, or used. The produce of those years is called Orlah. In the fourth year it is holy and dedicated to the altar. Only from the fifth year onward does it belong fully to the farmer.

This is a commandment of **patience and restraint**. The tree grows in front of you. The fruit is real and ripe and yours by every physical measure — but not yet yours by covenant.

---

## Player Experience

Orlah fires when the player has grown a fruit crop (grapes, figs, pomegranates, olives) for the first time, or when a young tree plot reaches its first yield. The narrative establishes that this is a young tree — its fruit is technically ready but falls under the Orlah restriction.

The dilemma lands with maximum weight when the player is **resource-constrained**. A player flush with wheat won't feel the pull. The ideal trigger is a lean season. The fruit is there. Leaving it feels wasteful. Taking it feels small.

The question the dilemma poses: *Can you wait when waiting costs you something real?*

---

## Moral Framing

Unlike social commandments (Peah, Leket) where sacrifice benefits someone visible, Orlah's restraint has no immediate visible recipient. You're not giving to the poor — you're simply not taking. The fruit falls and rots. The act of refraining is between the farmer and the land itself, and whatever principle governs both.

This makes Orlah feel more private and more spiritually oriented than economically oriented. Players in "national framing" mode can read it as a covenant with the land — a discipline of delayed ownership. Players in "religious framing" mode read it in its traditional sense.

---

## Choices

**A — Leave the fruit; the tree isn't yours to harvest yet**
"Three years of growth, and not a bite. You let the fruit fall untouched."
- No wheat gained (prevents the harvest)
- Devotion ++, Faithfulness +
- Flavor text: "The fruit drops. The earth takes it back. Next year, it's yours."

**B — Take the fruit — it's right there and you're hungry**
"The fruit won't wait, and neither will your family. You harvest it."
- Wheat/resource gained from the yield (normal harvest amount)
- Devotion −−, Faithfulness −
- Flavor text is not condemning: "The fruit is in your hands. It tastes like it always does. You push the thought aside."

**C — Take only a small amount, trying to minimize the transgression**
"You take just enough to ease the week. Not everything. Not nothing."
- Partial resource gain (half yield)
- Devotion −, Faithfulness −
- This choice exists only if the player's wheat is below a threshold (contextual option)

---

## Trigger Conditions

- Fires when a tree crop reaches its first harvestable state (tied to the tree/orchard mechanic when implemented)
- As a POC approximation before trees exist: fires once per game year on the first harvest of a designated crop, with narrative establishing it as a young tree
- Should not cycle frequently — Orlah is a multi-year constraint, not a per-harvest event; its rarity gives it weight

---

## Tone Notes

- The narrative should acknowledge the material cost openly — no pretense that abstaining is easy
- Choice B should not be framed as failure; the player is human, not a saint
- Visual: a lush, ripe-looking tree tile that the player *cannot* tap without the dilemma appearing — the visual temptation is part of the experience
- If the player picks Choice B: no dramatic punishment, but a quiet acknowledgment; the moral cost is internal, not spectacular

---

## Connection to Other Features

- **Additional crops / tree crops** — Orlah becomes a natural mechanic once fruit trees (grapes, olives, figs) are added; it creates a 3-cycle waiting period before yield, giving long-form investment meaning
- **Crop seasons / soil mechanics** — a young tree is a multi-year commitment; Orlah creates organic tension between long-term farming and short-term need
- **Meters and consequences** — repeated Orlah violations degrade Devotion in a compounding way; a player who always takes early will eventually see a threshold event
- **Dilemma history log** — Orlah entries in the log tell a story about discipline vs. pragmatism across seasons
